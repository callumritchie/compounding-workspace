# Central Tension — Acme Health

## The core tension
**Operational ambition vs. financial risk discipline** — embodied in the
COO–CFO split over how to move on value-based care.

The two leaders agree on the *destination* (value-based care) but disagree
fundamentally on the *evidence bar required to commit*:

| | COO (Operations) | CFO (Finance) |
|---|---|---|
| Stance | Enthusiastic champion | Open but sceptical |
| Readiness view | "Clinically we're ready — 80% there" | Assumes the smooth transition won't hold |
| Decision rule | "If we wait for a perfect forecast we'll never move — pilot and learn" | "No sensitivity analysis, the answer is no" |
| Bottleneck (self-diagnosed) | Finance's risk appetite | Non-credible downside cases |
| Blind spot | Light on downside quantification | Could stall momentum waiting for certainty |

## Why it matters
- The CFO is the **economic buyer** and holds a hard veto: no credible
  downside/stress case → no sign-off. Any recommendation ignoring this fails
  regardless of upside.
- The COO is the day-to-day stakeholder and the source of the feasibility
  narrative — but hasn't quantified what happens "in the year it isn't smooth."
- The board wants a recommendation in 8 weeks with a **clear financial case**,
  so the tension must be resolved, not papered over.

## The likely bridge
A **two-site pilot** is the candidate mechanism to reconcile the two:
- Gives the COO the "learn by doing" momentum he wants.
- *Could* give the CFO empirical downside protection instead of modelled
  assumptions.
- **Open question / untested:** Would the CFO actually accept a pilot as
  sufficient downside protection? (Hypothesis confidence: low.)

## Implication for the recommendation
Lead with a **credible downside/sensitivity case**, then frame the pilot as the
risk-management instrument that satisfies finance while unlocking operations —
positioning the pilot as a de-risking tool, not a delay.
